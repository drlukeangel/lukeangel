The role of an **Agile Product Owner** is — without exaggeration — the highest-leverage and worst-understood seat on most agile teams. The team's velocity, the customer's happiness, the engineering org's sanity: all three sit on whether the PO is making good calls. And yet most teams hire a PO the way they hire a project manager who happens to know JIRA. *That is the bug.* This course is the fix.

## What's in the box

The course works from the absolute fundamentals up to the strategic stuff. Eighteen segments, structured so the boring foundational topics are short and the high-leverage ones get real airtime:

- **Product Owner 101** — where the role actually came from, and why your project manager isn't one (no matter what their LinkedIn says)
- **Successful Product Owner habits** — collaboration patterns with engineering, design, and the business
- **Product Vision & Roadmap** — connecting strategy to the next sprint without faking a Gantt chart
- **Value & Risk** — how to weigh both on a single backlog without a spreadsheet
- **MVP, Prototyping, A/B Testing** — picking the smallest honest learning experiment
- **Backlog Grooming** — the unglamorous weekly habit that makes everything else work
- **Avoiding Agile Pitfalls** — the half-dozen ways product owners predictably fail and how to spot the warning signs early

The full syllabus (and a few bonus chapters) lives on the course page.

## The five PO failure modes the course teaches you to spot early

**The "ticket clerk" PO.** Spends 90% of the week writing JIRA tickets to engineering's exact spec. Nothing about value, strategy, or customer. Course module 3 reframes this — the PO writes the *outcome*, engineering owns the *implementation*.

**The "yes-to-everyone" PO.** Lets the loudest stakeholder dominate the roadmap. The backlog becomes a wishlist instead of a strategy. Module 7 teaches the "no, here's what we *will* ship" conversation pattern.

**The "absent" PO.** Comes to refinement but never to standup, never to demos, never to retro. Engineering loses trust because every decision takes 48 hours. Module 9: PO presence cadences for distributed and co-located teams.

**The "release manager in disguise" PO.** Treats the sprint like a deadline, ships features by date instead of value. The acceptance criteria become checkboxes. Module 12 covers the *outcomes vs outputs* distinction with three real-world contrasts.

**The "MVP means cheap" PO.** Builds the minimum viable product but skips the *viable* part. Ships things that don't actually test the hypothesis. Module 14 walks the five smallest honest learning experiments — paper prototype, concierge, Wizard of Oz, smoke test, fake door.

## The single best PO interview question

"Tell me about a feature you killed. What signal made you kill it, and how did you communicate it to the team that built it?"

If they can't answer this concretely, **they've never said no to anything**, which means they're a project manager with a different title.
